What is Social Psychology?
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Page 3

Social Psychology studies how people's thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of others.

Basic assumptions:

  • We are social beings ‚Äî we don't exist in isolation
  • Our behaviour is shaped by social situations, not just personality
  • We are influenced by others even when they're not physically present

Key topics in social psychology:

  • Conformity ‚Äî fitting in with the group
  • Obedience ‚Äî following orders from authority
  • Compliance ‚Äî agreeing to requests
  • Minority influence ‚Äî how small groups change majority opinion
üåç Real World Connection

Ever changed your opinion after seeing what your friends think on social media? That's social psychology in action. You might have had a strong view on something, but after seeing everyone else's comments, you started doubting yourself or even changed your mind. Our thoughts aren't as "independent" as we like to think.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Social psychology is all about how other people affect us. It explains why you might act differently when you're alone versus when you're in a group. It's why peer pressure works, why we follow trends, and why authority figures can make us do things we wouldn't normally do.

Conformity: Types & Reasons
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 4‚Äì5

Conformity = when an individual's behaviour is influenced by a larger or more dominant group.

Three types of conformity (Kelman, 1958):

  • Compliance ‚Äî publicly going along with the group but privately disagreeing. You don't want to look different.
  • Identification ‚Äî conforming because you value the group. Public change, but not fully agreeing internally.
  • Internalisation ‚Äî genuinely accepting the group's views as your own. Both public and private change.

Why do we conform? Two reasons:

  • Informational social influence ‚Äî we look to the group for guidance when we're uncertain. We assume they have better information. Common in ambiguous situations.
  • Normative social influence ‚Äî we don't want to be rejected or look foolish, so we follow the group norms even if we think they're wrong.
üåç Real World Connection

Think about fashion trends. When everyone starts wearing a certain style, you might start wearing it too ‚Äî even if you don't personally love it. That's normative influence (you don't want to look out of place). But if you're in a new city and everyone's queuing at a particular food stall, you might join the queue thinking "they must know it's good" ‚Äî that's informational influence.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Conformity is just changing your behaviour to match the group. Sometimes you do it because you think the group knows better than you (informational). Sometimes you do it because you don't want to stand out or be judged (normative). The type of conformity depends on whether you're just pretending to agree or actually changing your mind.

Asch & Sherif: Conformity Studies
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 6‚Äì7

Asch (1951) ‚Äî Normative Influence

  • Aim: To see if people conform on an unambiguous (clear) task
  • Method: Participants judged line lengths. Confederates (fake participants) deliberately gave wrong answers
  • Results: 75% of participants conformed at least once. Wrong answers given 37% of the time (vs 0.7% when alone)
  • Why? Participants said they didn't believe the wrong answer but didn't want to appear different ‚Äî normative influence

Sherif (1935) ‚Äî Informational Influence

  • Aim: To see if people conform on an ambiguous (unclear) task
  • Method: Used autokinetic effect (stationary light appears to move in dark room). Participants judged how far it "moved"
  • Results: When alone, everyone had different estimates. In groups, estimates converged (became similar). When retested alone, participants stuck with the group norm
  • Why? The task was ambiguous, so participants genuinely looked to others for information ‚Äî informational influence
Asch's line task ‚Äî the correct answer (B) was obvious
üåç Real World Connection

Asch's study explains why people go along with clearly wrong answers in meetings ‚Äî nobody wants to be the one person disagreeing with everyone else. Sherif's study explains why we check reviews before buying something ‚Äî when we're uncertain, we look to what others think to guide us.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Asch: Even when the answer is obvious, people will say something wrong just to fit in with the group. Sherif: When the answer isn't clear, people genuinely change their minds to match the group because they assume the group knows better. Different reasons for conforming.

Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Page 8

Zimbardo et al. (1973)

  • Aim: To test conformity to assigned social roles
  • Method: 24 male college students randomly assigned as "prisoners" or "guards" in a mock prison (Stanford University basement). Guards given freedom to maintain order but no physical violence allowed. Planned for 2 weeks.
  • Results:
    • Guards became increasingly authoritarian and abusive
    • Prisoners showed psychological distress, passivity, emotional breakdowns
    • Deindividuation occurred ‚Äî people stopped feeling like individuals
    • Experiment stopped after only 6 days due to ethical concerns
  • Similar findings: Orlando (1973) mock psychiatric ward
üåç Real World Connection

This study helps explain real-world abuses of power ‚Äî like prison guard brutality or workplace bullying. When people are given a role (especially an authority role), they often conform to what they think that role should be, even if it means acting cruelly. The uniform and the situation can overpower someone's normal personality. It's terrifying and important.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Normal, healthy students were randomly assigned to be guards or prisoners. Within days, the "guards" became abusive and the "prisoners" broke down emotionally. Nobody told them to act this way ‚Äî they just conformed to the role they were given. It shows how powerful situations can be in shaping behaviour, often more powerful than personality.

Fake News & Conformity Online
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Page 9

Colliander (2019)

  • Aim: To investigate conformity effects when people respond to fake news on social media
  • Method: Independent measures design ‚Äî 4 groups exposed to fake news article:
    • Group 1: Article with no comments
    • Group 2: Article with positive comments
    • Group 3: Article with critical comments
    • Group 4: Article with positive comments + disclaimer saying it's fake
  • Results:
    • After seeing critical comments, people were less likely to share or positively comment
    • After seeing positive comments, people were more likely to engage positively
    • Surprisingly: the disclaimer from the social media platform did not reduce sharing/commenting
üåç Real World Connection

This explains why fake news spreads so easily on social media. If you see an article with hundreds of positive comments, you're more likely to believe it and share it ‚Äî even if it's completely false. We conform to what other commenters are saying. Worryingly, official "fact check" warnings don't work as well as you'd think ‚Äî peer comments are more influential than platform warnings.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

We're heavily influenced by what other people say in the comments, not just by the content itself. If everyone's praising an article, we're more likely to share it. If everyone's criticising it, we hold back. The platform telling us "this is fake" matters less than what our peers are saying. That's conformity in the digital age.

Minority Influence
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 10‚Äì13

Minority Influence ‚Äî small groups or individuals can change how the majority thinks.

Moscovici's Conversion Theory:

  • Moscovici argued Asch focused too much on majority ‚Üí minority influence
  • The opposite is also possible: minority ‚Üí majority
  • Conversion = how a minority convinces the majority their views are correct
  • Unlike compliance (public change only), conversion involves both public and private change
  • Minority influence is based on informational influence ‚Äî providing new ideas that make the majority re-evaluate

Moscovici et al. (1969) Blue-Green Study:

  • Method: 2 confederates + 4 real participants. Shown 36 blue slides, asked to call out the colour
    • Condition 1: Confederates said "green" on all 36 slides (consistent)
    • Condition 2: Confederates said "green" 24 times, "blue" 12 times (inconsistent)
  • Results: Consistent minority influenced majority 8.42% of the time. Inconsistent minority only 1.25%. 32% of participants said "green" at least once.
  • Key finding: Consistency is crucial for minority influence
üåç Real World Connection

Think about social movements ‚Äî women's suffrage, civil rights, climate activism. These all started as minority views that seemed extreme at the time. But because activists were consistent in their message over years, they gradually convinced the majority. Greta Thunberg is a modern example ‚Äî one teenager's consistent message influenced millions.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Minorities can change majority opinion, but only if they're consistent. If a small group keeps saying the same thing confidently over time, the majority starts to think "maybe they have a point." But if the minority keeps changing their message or contradicting themselves, nobody takes them seriously. Consistency = credibility.

The consistent minority was nearly 7 times more influential than the inconsistent one. When the minority kept changing (blue/green/blue/green), people dismissed them. But when they stuck to "green" every single time, some participants started questioning their own perception. Consistency signals confidence and commitment, which makes the majority take the minority seriously.
Compliance Techniques
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 14‚Äì16

Compliance = a change in behaviour in response to requests (not orders). Usually superficial, public, and temporary.

Foot-in-the-Door (Freedman & Fraser, 1966):

  • Agreeing to a small request increases likelihood of agreeing to a larger request later
  • Consistency is key ‚Äî larger request must be similar to the small one
  • Example (Sherman, 1980): Called people asking if they'd hypothetically volunteer 3 hours for charity. 3 days later, asked them to actually volunteer ‚Üí 31% agreed (vs 4% who only got the second call)

Door-in-the-Face (Cialdini et al., 1975):

  • Refusing a large request increases likelihood of accepting a smaller request
  • Reciprocity is important ‚Äî when someone makes a concession, we feel obliged to reciprocate
  • Example (Cialdini, 1975): Asked people to be counsellors for 2 years (most refused), then asked them to take kids to zoo once ‚Üí 50% agreed (vs much lower baseline)
üåç Real World Connection

Foot-in-the-door: Charities often ask you to sign a petition first (small request), then ask for a donation (larger request). Salespeople offer a "free trial" knowing once you've started using something, you're more likely to buy. Door-in-the-face: Estate agents show you an overpriced house first, then show you a "more reasonable" one ‚Äî suddenly it seems like a bargain even though it's still expensive.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Foot-in-the-door: Get someone to say yes to something small, and they're more likely to say yes to something bigger later. We like to be consistent. Door-in-the-face: Ask for something huge (which gets rejected), then ask for something smaller ‚Äî people feel like they should meet you halfway. Both techniques manipulate our desire to seem consistent or fair.

Milgram's Obedience Study
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 17‚Äì21

Obedience = acting in response to a direct order from an authority figure.

Different from conformity (social pressure) ‚Äî obedience involves a hierarchy of power.

Milgram (1963) ‚Äî Famous obedience study:

  • Setting: Yale University lab. 40 men participated
  • Cover story: Told it was a study on learning and memory
  • Method: Participant = "teacher," confederate = "learner." Teacher told to give electric shocks for wrong answers, increasing from 15V to 450V. Learner strapped in chair (fake shocks, real protests)
  • Results:
    • 65% (26/40) went to 450V maximum shock
    • 100% went past 300V (when learner protested loudly)

Gibson et al. (2017) reanalysis:

  • Analyzed audio recordings from Milgram's study
  • Found participants used "just following orders" as a strategy to justify their behaviour, not just blind obedience
  • Many didn't want to continue but did so because they were told to
Setup: Experimenter (authority) orders teacher (participant) to shock learner (confederate)
üåç Real World Connection

Milgram's study was designed to understand how ordinary Germans obeyed orders during the Holocaust. The terrifying finding? 65% of ordinary Americans also obeyed destructive orders in a lab. This isn't about "evil people" ‚Äî it's about how situations and authority can make normal people do terrible things. It explains atrocities from Nazi Germany to Abu Ghraib prison.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

Milgram showed that most people will follow orders from an authority figure, even when those orders involve harming someone. The participants weren't sadists ‚Äî many were visibly distressed ‚Äî but they kept going because someone in a lab coat told them to. Authority is incredibly powerful, sometimes more powerful than our own conscience.

Agency Theory & Milgram Variations
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 23‚Äì24

Milgram's Agency Theory (1973):

  • Agentic state ‚Äî when people act as someone's agent, not taking personal responsibility. They're just "following orders."
  • Opposite = autonomous state ‚Äî acting independently, taking responsibility
  • Binding factors keep people in agentic state:
    • Reluctance to disrupt the experiment
    • Pressure of the surroundings (lab setting)
    • Insistence of the authority figure

Factors affecting obedience (Milgram's variations):

  • Presence of allies: If others disobey, obedience drops dramatically
  • Proximity to victim: Closer to victim = less obedience (e.g., same room vs different room)
  • Proximity of authority: Closer to authority = more obedience (phone orders = less obedience)
  • Location legitimacy: Yale (prestigious) = more obedience than run-down office
üåç Real World Connection

The agentic state explains why soldiers can commit war crimes ‚Äî they shift responsibility to their commanding officer. It also explains corporate wrongdoing ("I was just doing my job"). The variations explain why whistleblowers are so rare ‚Äî if your boss (authority) is right there and nobody else is objecting (no allies), it's incredibly hard to disobey, even when you know something's wrong.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

When we're following orders, we mentally shift responsibility onto the authority figure ‚Äî "it's not my fault, I was told to do it." This agentic state makes it easier to do harmful things. Obedience is strongest when the authority is close, the victim is far away, and nobody else is refusing. Change any of those factors and obedience drops.

Obedience: What Increases/Decreases It?
üìò Concept
üìÑ Lecture slides: Pages 25‚Äì26

What makes obedience MORE likely:

  • Gradual commitment ‚Äî agreeing to small steps makes it harder to refuse the next request (like foot-in-the-door)
  • Legitimate authority ‚Äî we're more likely to obey people we see as justified authorities (police, doctors, teachers, parents)
    • Bickman (1974): Field experiment where experimenters in different uniforms asked pedestrians to do tasks. Uniform = more obedience

What makes us RESISTANT to obedience:

  • Dissenting peers ‚Äî seeing others refuse makes it easier for you to refuse too
  • Higher moral reasoning ‚Äî people with stronger personal moral codes find it harder to shift responsibility
  • Locus of control (Rotter, 1966):
    • Internal locus = believe you control your own actions ‚Üí more resistant to obedience
    • External locus = believe outside forces control you ‚Üí more obedient
    • Holland (1967): Found link between internal locus of control and resistance to authority in Milgram-style studies
üåç Real World Connection

Legitimate authority matters: During COVID-19, people were more likely to follow rules when they came from doctors/scientists (legitimate health authority) than politicians. Con artists exploit this ‚Äî they wear fake uniforms or use official-sounding titles to manipulate people. Dissenting peers matter: In corrupt organizations, one whistleblower makes it easier for others to speak up. The first person to refuse is incredibly brave because they have no allies.

üñäÔ∏è Plain English

We obey more when authority seems legitimate (uniforms, titles, settings) and when we're committed gradually (small steps leading to big ones). We obey less when someone else refuses first (gives us permission to refuse too) or when we have a strong internal sense of right and wrong that won't let us shift responsibility.

üìù Summary

Key takeaways from Social Psychology:

  • Conformity: We change behaviour to match groups (normative or informational influence)
  • Minority influence: Small groups can change majority opinion if they're consistent
  • Compliance: Foot-in-the-door and door-in-the-face techniques manipulate our desire for consistency/reciprocity
  • Obedience: Most people obey authority figures, even to harmful extremes (Milgram 65%)
  • Agentic state: We shift responsibility to authority when following orders
  • Resistance: Dissenting peers, internal locus of control, and strong moral reasoning help us resist
